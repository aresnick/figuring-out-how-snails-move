How does a snail move?

The way snails move turns out to be pretty unusual. We're going to try to understand it by analogy‚Äî First thinking about ourselves, then inchworms, then earthworms, before finally looking at an actual snail. But before we get to any of that, let's start with thinking about how things move, period‚Äî

So how do things move? Well, every thing has weight. Because it has some weight, to move it we have to push on it. But how do we push on it? Weirdly, the only way we can push on something is by pushing on something else, and having that something else push back on us.

What?

Well, think about how you walk. Imagine you're just standing there.

How do you take a step?

Well first you lift your foot.

Then you move that lifted foot forward, moving your whole body a little bit forward at the same time.

When you moved your leg forward, you had to "push" on something. Imagine floating in space and taking a step. Your leg would move, but you wouldn't.

Why not? Well, because when you take a step without actually standing on anything, you're just moving your legs around.

Imagine one of your feet is "stuck" to the earth; when you try to move your other leg, your stuck leg doesn't move. So not only does your other leg move forward, but the rest of your body is pulled/pushed along with it.

Then what happens? Your lifted foot hits the ground and it gets stuck.

Then, you lift your back foot, and move it forward‚Äî¬†Now, your front (stuck) foot is pulling your back leg (and body) forward.

Then, your back foot hits the ground, becomes the front foot, and the whole process starts all over again. And then you're walking!

So fundamentally, for something to move along the ground, we need to have it push on something, extend or move itself, grab hold further along its path, and use its new hold to move again.

It's pretty easy to see how we do this with two feet. But what if you don't have legs?

Let's think about an inchworm. An inchworm doesn't have legs, right?

Well if we look up close at an inchworm, we see it actually kind of does‚Äî¬†An inchworm keeps its rear "legs" stuck, then stretches itself forward,

then sticks its "front" legs, lifts its "rear", squinches up,

and repeats.

Other questions which came up observing snails

I'd never really thought about snails before today, much less looked up close at them. A handful of questions came to mind before I decided to settle on and observe the snail's locomotion. Here are a few of them.

  • What is the relative proportion of force applied to the mucus by the rim versus the muscles along the length of the snail?
  • What's the relationship between the speed of the waves and the speed of the snail?
  • The waves seem to originate in the tail‚Äî¬†Do all snails do that? Is there an advantage to that?
  • How do the two types of mucus (sticky and lubricating) mix under the pedal of the snail?
  • What does the snail smell with? It seemed, for example, to respond to lettuce.
  • How do muscles in invertebrae animals work? Like what do they pull and push against? When the snail lifts its head, how does it do that?
  • Why does the rim of the snail sometimes "pucker", with a small wrinkle that seems to stay stationary, even as the snail is moving?
  • What was the evolutionary precedent for this wacky, wave motion?
  • What's the internal anatomy of the snail's muscles? Do the muscles generating the waves live on the bottom of the foot, or is the whole snail that's contracting?
  • Can you tell the age of a snail from its shell?
  • Does what a snail eats change the color of its shell?
  • How high is the resolution of the non-optical photoreceptors on a snail's skin?
  • How similar is a snail's motion to, e.g. a snake's?
  • Can snails hear?
  • About how viscous is snail's slimes? What is it comparable to in my daily life?
  • How can other snail's understand the direction of motion from a slime trail?
  • What does a snail eating sound like?
  • If I feed a snail something luminescent, could I watch it go through its digestive tract?

Feedback on Molly's 2 August studio session

At Molly's request, I've collated some of my notes as to what might improve similar experiences in future workshops here, in no particular order:

  • The ergonomics of sitting at a table and recording snails isn't great. Having snails at eye-level somehow (and/or rolling, adjustable-height stools) would help.
  • Related‚Äîespecially in bringing in new people to do this type of work‚ÄîI think it could be a lot more fun to partner up and have people talk/hang out with each other as they record. This could help in noticing, but also in basic videography.
  • Rather than watching/helping us video, I think it would have been more fun if Molly had done the work alongside us.
  • Especially given snails' sensitivity to heat, it might be nice to have easy, bright, but cool (i.e. LED) lighting available for filming.
  • I made the mistake of jumping straight in trying to photograph and record the snails. Really, I think it would have been good to just spend 15‚Äì30 minutes just hanging out with the snails, watching them, talking about them, and maybe learning a bit about how to move them around (e.g. with lettuce, or slime, or similar).
  • It would have been nice to begin with a clear example of what we were aiming to create‚Äîeven if you emphasized that we could do something else. That would help in brainstorming and thinking about what to pay attention to.
  • Keeping my hands steady was hard. Given how much you want to move to accommodate a snail's motion, I wonder whether a steadicam or monopod might be of use?
  • Having a nice piece of flat glass‚Äîmaybe a few L-shaped pieces too‚Äîwould be convenient, especially if you'd thought through how to make it easy to video.
  • Producing video content with a nice background would be a lot easier if we thought a bit about staging, or having thin sheets of oak tag or similar around.

So to convert a GIF to a sequence of PNGs, you can select all but one of the frames, drag those out, and then drag the last frame out. This gives you a collection of TIFFs which you can then use mogrify to convert.
  • In creating my walking GIFs, I wanted to remove the white background from a bunch of images. This StackOverflow post was very helpful in figuring out how to do that.

  • mogrify was a very easy way to convert the format of a bunch of images at once.
  • This StackOverflow post was an easy way to generate thumbnails for my media archive.
